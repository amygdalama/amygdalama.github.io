Draft

March 08 2014

This is a draft post.

Things I'm happy with: I had made some last minute changes, adding the tic tac toe example and the explanations involving code objects and bytecode. I received positive feedback on each of these additions, so I think I made the right choice. The part I liked most is that these added some threads that curious people could pull on to learn more. At least half the audience got at least one trivia question wrong! Which means they learned something! My explanations clear, rigorous, and fun! Some people asked questions! I'm assuming this means they found the topic interesting! The first question I got during the Q&A wasn't a question, and instead was a claim that I said something incorrect. Many people have given me positive feedback on how I handled that situation. My response was, in Allison Kaptur's words, "inviting, informative, and authoritative." I admitted I didn't know an answer to a question! I'm proud of myself for being brave enough to admit this and for setting an example that Not Knowing Everything is totally okay. Ned Batchelder gave me a shoutout during his talk! I was comfortable enough to watch the video shortly after my talk, and I'm really happy with it! * I didn't use any words I've been trying to eliminate from my vocabulary ("crazy" is one that I was worried about in particular)!

Things I hope to get better at: I'd like to give a more advanced talk. I keep comparing myself to people who have given many Python talks, have been programming much longer than I have, etc, and I should stop that. Pause and drink water between sections/examples. Figure out what makes some talks more popular than others. My talk didn't have the turnout I was hoping for, and I'm not sure why or how to get better at that yet.
